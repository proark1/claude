01Start with a real reason

Pick one gathering you can picture.

Not “an event-management platform.” Think: Sunday lunch, a birthday walk, five friends at the cinema. Familiar details make your instructions sharper.

The one-sentence test

“Gather helps a group agree on when to meet, say who is coming, and divide the small jobs.” If an idea does not support that sentence, leave it for later.

  • One organizer creates the event
  • Guests respond without learning a new system
  • Everyone sees the same current plan

04What Gather remembers

Describe the information before the database.

A is simply an agreement about what exists and how it connects.

Data conversation
Before adding a database, explain Gather’s proposed data model in plain language. Show one realistic example event with guests, three date options, votes, and tasks. Point out duplicate or missing information. Wait for my approval before creating database files.
Common trap

Saving the final date too early

Date options and the chosen final date are different things. Ask Claude to keep both so the app can show voting first and the decision later.

07People change their minds

Edit freely. Delete carefully.

Real plans move. Guests switch dates, tasks change hands, and accidental taps happen. Gather should make ordinary changes easy and irreversible ones obvious.

Low-risk action

Filtering “coming” guests or marking a task complete can happen immediately and be easy to reverse.

High-risk action

Deleting the whole event deserves a clear description, confirmation, and preferably a recovery path.

Safety prompt
Add editing, RSVP filters, task completion, and deletion. Keep reversible actions quick. Before destructive actions, state exactly what will disappear and require confirmation. Add an empty state that tells the organizer what to do next. Test cancel as well as confirm.
